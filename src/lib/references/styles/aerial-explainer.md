# aerial-explainer

Drone-view architectural storytelling. A building, space, or environment rendered as an isometric cutaway or bird's-eye view — walls removed to reveal the interior. Numbered annotations explain zones, features, flow, and function. The space itself IS the story.

## Design Philosophy

Imagine hovering above a building with X-ray vision. You see every room, every aisle, every pathway — how people move through the space, where things happen, what makes it work. Like the "Is Everything Super in the Supermarket?" cutaway, the NYT Greenwood District aerial, or the White House architectural breakdown. The illustration is the star — 60-70% of the canvas. Text is organized cleanly around it with numbered markers connecting points on the illustration to their descriptions.

## The Illustration — Isometric Architectural Cutaway

This is the HERO — 60-70% of the canvas area.

### Perspective
- **Isometric bird's-eye view** at roughly 30-45 degree angle — like a drone hovering above and to one side
- The viewer looks DOWN into the space — roofs removed, walls cut away, interior visible
- Show DEPTH — multiple levels, rooms behind rooms, outdoor areas connecting to indoor
- The angle should reveal the spatial relationships — how spaces connect, how people flow through

### Rendering Style
- **Clean architectural illustration** — precise geometry, accurate proportions, detailed interiors
- NOT cartoon, NOT sketch — think architectural rendering meets editorial illustration
- Interior details visible: furniture, objects, products, equipment, people (tiny figures showing scale and activity)
- Walls rendered as clean cuts — the cutaway edge should be crisp, showing wall thickness and materials
- Slight shadows and depth cues to create dimensionality
- The building should feel REAL and INHABITED — not an empty shell

### Color Treatment
- **Building structure**: Rendered in warm, realistic but slightly stylized tones — cream/stone walls, wood floors, tile, concrete. NOT gray wireframe. The materials should be recognizable.
- **Interior zones**: Each major zone/area can have a subtle color tint to distinguish it — warm amber for food areas, cool blue for tech zones, green for garden/nature, etc. The tinting is SUBTLE — just enough to help zone identification, not overwhelming.
- **Accent highlights**: Key features or focal points get a POP of saturated color — a bright red product, a golden chandelier, a blue pool. These draw the eye to important elements.
- **People/figures**: Small but visible, shown in action — shopping, walking, working. They give LIFE and SCALE to the space.
- **Background**: Clean white or very light warm gray. The building floats on the page.

### What to Show Inside
- Furniture, fixtures, equipment appropriate to the space
- Products on shelves, food on tables, art on walls — the CONTENTS that make the space what it is
- People engaged in activities — this shows HOW the space is used
- Pathways, corridors, doorways — the FLOW through the space
- Architectural details — columns, arches, stairs, windows, courtyards
- Outdoor elements connecting to the building — gardens, parking, entrance paths

## Numbered Annotation System

THIS IS THE KEY INTERACTION MECHANISM. Numbered markers on the illustration connect to text descriptions.

### Markers
- Small numbered circles (1, 2, 3... up to 10-14) placed directly on the illustration at key points
- Circle style: bold number inside a filled circle in a consistent accent color (e.g., warm gold #C49A3C, deep teal #2A7B6B, or vermillion #C0392B — pick ONE color for all markers)
- Markers should be large enough to read but not cover important illustration detail
- Place markers at the ZONE or FEATURE they describe — inside the room, at the entrance, on the product

### Marker-to-Text Connection
- Each numbered marker corresponds to a numbered text block in the margins
- The connection is by NUMBER — no callout lines needed (unlike deconstruct style). The numbered system is clean and self-explanatory.
- Optionally, thin subtle leader lines can connect markers to text when they're far apart — but keep these minimal

### Text Blocks
Each numbered point gets:
1. **Number + Bold header** (3-6 words): e.g., "1 SIZE MATTERS" or "7 FRESHLY BAKED BREAD MAKES HUNGRY"
2. **Short description** (2-3 sentences max): Conversational, engaging explanation of what happens at this point and WHY it matters. Not dry specs — tell the story.

Format: Number is in the accent color, header is bold, description is regular weight. Keep it SHORT.

## Text and Layout

### Title
- LARGE, bold, editorial. Can be a question ("Is Everything Super in the Supermarket?") or declarative ("Inside Qasr Al Hosn").
- Serif or strong sans-serif — authoritative but approachable.
- Placed at the top, left-aligned or centered. Should dominate the header zone.
- Color: dark charcoal (#1A1A1A) or deep brown (#3E2723).

### Subtitle
- 2-3 sentences of context below the title. Regular weight, warm gray.
- Sets up WHY this space matters and what the reader will discover.

### Numbered Descriptions
- Organized in the margins around the illustration — left side, right side, bottom
- Each entry: NUMBER (accent color, large) + HEADER (bold, dark) + DESCRIPTION (regular, warm brown)
- Keep descriptions to 2-3 SHORT sentences. Anti-squeeze rule applies.
- Text blocks should align cleanly — not scattered randomly

### Stats / Key Facts (optional)
- A small data panel or footer strip with key numbers about the space
- Square footage, year built, capacity, visitors per year, etc.
- Clean, readable, not tiny

## Typography — Clean and Readable

- **Title**: VERY LARGE, bold serif or strong sans-serif, dark. The hero text.
- **Subtitle**: Medium-large, regular weight, warm gray. Engaging context.
- **Number markers**: LARGE bold in accent color inside circles on the illustration.
- **Point headers**: Medium-large, **bold**, dark charcoal. Numbered. Short and punchy.
- **Point descriptions**: Medium, *regular weight*, clean sans-serif, warm brown. NEVER bold. 2-3 SHORT sentences max.
- **Stats / captions**: Medium, regular, warm gray.

Weight gradient: Bold title → bold numbered headers → light descriptions. NOT everything bold.

## Composition

### Layout Zones
1. **Header zone** (top 8-10%): Title, subtitle, context
2. **Illustration zone** (center 60-70%): The isometric cutaway architectural rendering with numbered markers placed ON the illustration
3. **Annotation zones** (sides and bottom): Numbered text blocks organized in clean columns
4. **Footer** (bottom 5%): Key stats, source, attribution

### Information Flow
1. Title grabs attention
2. The aerial view draws the eye — it's visually rich and detailed
3. Numbered markers catch the eye — the reader picks a number
4. They find the matching numbered description in the margins
5. They read the short explanation
6. They return to the illustration to find the next number
7. It's an INTERACTIVE visual experience — illustration and text work together

### White Space
- The illustration is dense with detail — that's expected and good
- But the TEXT AREAS must have generous white space — clean margins, breathing room
- Don't cram descriptions together. Let each numbered point have its own space.

## What This Is

- An architectural explainer that shows HOW A SPACE WORKS from above
- Isometric cutaway revealing interiors, flow, and function
- Numbered annotation system connecting illustration to text
- Clean, detailed, inhabited — the space feels REAL and ALIVE
- Like a page from an architecture magazine or a NYT visual feature
- The viewer UNDERSTANDS the space after looking at it — layout, purpose, flow

## What This Is NOT

- NOT an exploded/deconstructed parts view (that's the deconstruct style)
- NOT a flat floorplan or blueprint — this is a 3D rendered view with depth
- NOT empty architecture — show people, objects, activity, LIFE
- NOT cluttered text — short numbered annotations, not paragraphs
- NOT dark background — clean white or very light
- NOT abstract or diagrammatic — the building should look like a REAL building

## Best For

Buildings and architecture, retail spaces, museums, airports, stadiums, historical sites, factories, offices, restaurants, hotels, hospitals, schools, any SPACE or ENVIRONMENT where understanding the layout explains the story. Also works for: city blocks, districts, campuses, ships, aircraft interiors, theme parks.
