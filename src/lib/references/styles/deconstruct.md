# deconstruct

Museum-quality editorial deconstruction. A central subject is pulled apart, exploded, or deconstructed on an aged parchment canvas. Each component is rendered in its OWN RICH PIGMENT COLOR — like hand-painted plates from a Victorian scientific atlas. Color IS the information layer. The presentation has the gravitas of a natural history museum exhibit and the precision of a New York Times visual explainer.

## Design Philosophy

Imagine a plate from Ernst Haeckel's "Art Forms in Nature" or a Da Vinci anatomical study — but with modern information design. The central subject is deconstructed and each part is rendered in a distinct, rich pigment color on warm parchment. The scholarly, museum-quality presentation gives the content AUTHORITY. The color-coding gives it CLARITY. It should feel like something you'd find framed in the Smithsonian or bound in a leather-covered scientific atlas.

## Color System — Pigment-Level Component Color Coding

THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT SECTION. Color is not decoration — it is INFORMATION.

### The Rule
Every major component/section of the deconstructed subject gets its OWN UNIQUE COLOR. That same color is used for:
1. The component in the central illustration
2. The section header text for that component
3. The callout line connecting the part to its label
4. Any highlighted text, metrics, or key data related to that component
5. Optional: a subtle tinted panel behind that section's text

### Color Palette (4-8 pigment colors)
Choose 4-8 rich, deep, distinguishable colors that feel like hand-ground pigments — NOT neon, NOT pastel, NOT flat digital. Think oil paint on canvas, natural dyes, mineral pigments. These are WARM and RICH, not cold and corporate:

Example palette for a mechanical subject (rocket, engine, device):
- Component 1: Vermillion red (#C0392B) — e.g., engines/propulsion
- Component 2: Prussian blue (#1A5276) — e.g., structural frame
- Component 3: Raw sienna / amber (#B7720E) — e.g., fuel system
- Component 4: Viridian green (#1E8449) — e.g., guidance/control
- Component 5: Tyrian purple (#6C3483) — e.g., payload/cargo
- Component 6: Burnt umber (#6E4B3A) — e.g., recovery system

Example palette for a biological subject (body, plant, cell):
- Component 1: Carmine rose (#A93226) — e.g., respiratory system
- Component 2: Indian yellow (#D4AC0D) — e.g., digestive organs
- Component 3: Malachite green (#1D8348) — e.g., intestinal system
- Component 4: Cobalt violet (#7D3C98) — e.g., renal/filtration
- Component 5: Cerulean blue (#2471A3) — e.g., circulatory system
- Component 6: Venetian red (#A04000) — e.g., musculoskeletal

### Background — Aged Parchment Canvas
The background is warm aged parchment — NOT white, NOT gray, NOT flat:
- Base tone: Warm parchment beige (#F0E0C4 → #E6D5B0) with gentle tonal variation
- Subtle paper grain texture throughout the surface
- Very slight darkening and warm toning toward edges and corners
- Faint foxing marks or age spots (subtle, atmospheric, NOT distracting)
- The parchment should feel REAL — like handmade cotton paper or aged vellum
- It provides warmth and gravitas that makes the colored components POP against it

### Non-component text
- **Title**: Deep sepia brown (#3E2723) or iron gall ink (#2C1810) — scholarly and authoritative
- **Body narrative**: Dark warm brown (#5D4037) — understated, elegant
- **Secondary labels**: Medium warm gray-brown (#8D6E63) — quiet
- **Callout dots**: Small filled circles in the component's pigment color

## The Central Illustration

This is the HEART of the infographic — 40-60% of the canvas area.

### Rendering Style
- Precise, detailed SCIENTIFIC ILLUSTRATION style — like a plate from a natural history atlas
- Think: Ernst Haeckel, Da Vinci anatomical sketches, Victorian engineering diagrams — but in color
- Fine ink line work with crosshatching and stippling for shadow and detail
- Isometric or 3/4 perspective view preferred — shows depth and structure
- CRITICAL: Each component is rendered in its ASSIGNED PIGMENT COLOR with fine ink outlines. Not flat color fills — use shading, crosshatching, and tonal variation WITHIN each color family to create depth and detail. The effect should be like hand-painted scientific plates where each organ or part is a distinct rich color.
- The illustration should feel hand-rendered — slightly organic line quality, as if drawn with a steel nib pen and colored with pigment washes

### Deconstruction Treatment
- **Exploded view**: Parts pulled apart along logical axes, floating in space. Each part in its own pigment color. Thin dashed lines showing where parts connect back together.
- **Cross-section / cutaway**: Slice revealing internal layers. Each layer in a different pigment color. The cut edge rendered with fine crosshatching.
- **Annotated whole**: Subject shown intact but with color-coded regions and callout lines radiating outward.
- Choose the method that best explains the subject.

### The Effect
When you look at the illustration, you should see 4-8 distinct pigment-colored zones against the warm parchment — like a beautifully hand-colored scientific plate. It should feel simultaneously ANCIENT in craft and MODERN in information clarity.

## Callout Lines and Labels

### Color-Coded Lines
- Each callout line uses the PIGMENT COLOR of the component it references
- Line starts with a small filled dot/circle in the component color
- Thin, precise lines rendered in the component color (0.75-1px)
- Straight segments with clean right-angle bends
- Lines should not cross — route them cleanly

### Color-Coded Section Headers
- Each component's section header is printed in THAT COMPONENT'S PIGMENT COLOR
- Example: "ENGINES & PROPULSION" in vermillion, "STRUCTURAL FRAME" in Prussian blue, "PAYLOAD" in Tyrian purple
- This is the CRITICAL visual link — the reader sees vermillion text and immediately finds the vermillion part

### Label Hierarchy — Weight GRADIENT, Not All Bold
CRITICAL: Create a clear GRADIENT of visual weight. NOT everything bold. The eye should flow naturally from heavy to light:

**Weight gradient (heaviest → lightest):**
1. **Component header**: LARGE, **bold** serif, IN COMPONENT COLOR. All caps. The ANCHOR.
2. **One-liner hook**: Medium-large, *medium weight* (NOT bold), component color at 70%. ONE single engaging sentence only.
3. **1-2 short body lines**: Medium, *regular/light weight*, clean sans-serif, warm brown (#6D5847). NOT bold. MAX 1-2 SHORT sentences. NEVER a paragraph.
4. **Key number callout**: LARGE, **bold**, component color. ONE hero number per component.
5. **2-3 callout labels**: Small-medium, *light weight*, warm gray (#8D7B6E). Short annotation labels only.

**NON-NEGOTIABLE ANTI-SQUEEZE RULE:**
- NEVER cram dense paragraphs into a section. If text doesn't fit comfortably with generous breathing room around it, CUT THE TEXT — do not shrink it or squeeze it.
- Each component section gets: 1 header + 1 hook sentence + 1-2 body sentences + 1 key number + 2-3 callout labels. THAT'S IT. No more.
- NO PARAGRAPHS. No blocks of 4+ lines of body text. If you wrote a paragraph, delete half of it.
- White space around text is SACRED. Text should float in space, not fill every pixel.
- The illustration does the heavy lifting. Text provides context — it does NOT try to explain everything in words.

The magic is CONTRAST: bold headers and numbers are the anchors, light short text is the breathing space between them. When everything is bold or dense, nothing stands out and nothing is readable.

## Text and Narrative

### Header Design — Formal, Scholarly, Museum-Quality
The header should feel like the title plate of a scientific atlas, a museum exhibition label, or the cover of a scholarly monograph:

- **Title**: VERY LARGE (4-5x body text), bold SERIF typeface — elegant, authoritative, classical. Think: Garamond, Caslon, Baskerville, or Didot aesthetic. Rendered in deep sepia brown (#3E2723) or iron gall ink (#2C1810). The title should be COMMANDING and impossible to miss.
- **Decorative treatment**: A thin ornamental rule or elegant hairline border above and below the title. Optional: small decorative flourish — subtle and tasteful.
- **Subtitle**: Elegant serif italic, LARGE size (1.5-2x body text), warm brown. Sets up the story with scholarly poise. Must be easily readable.
- **Byline / attribution**: Medium small caps in warm gray-brown.
- The header zone can have a slightly deeper parchment tint — like a title cartouche on an antique map.
- DO make it feel like opening a rare scientific book. DO NOT make it feel like a PowerPoint slide or corporate report.

### Typography — Weight Gradient + Digital Readability
The scholarly feel comes from typeface CHOICE, not from making everything bold. Create rhythm through a clear weight gradient:

**The weight gradient system:**
- **Title**: VERY LARGE, **BOLD** serif, deep sepia/ink. The heaviest text on the page.
- **Component headers**: LARGE, **BOLD** serif or small caps, IN COMPONENT PIGMENT COLOR. Clearly bigger than body text.
- **One-liner hooks**: Medium-large, *medium weight* (NOT bold), component color. ONE engaging sentence.
- **Body text**: Medium, *regular/light weight*, CLEAN SANS-SERIF, warm brown. NEVER bold. MAX 1-2 SHORT sentences. NEVER a paragraph.
- **Key numbers**: LARGE, **BOLD**, component color. Numbers POP against the light body text.
- **Stats bar**: Medium, *regular weight*, warm brown.

**The flow**: BOLD header → light one-liner → light short body → BOLD number → next header. It BREATHES. Heavy-light-heavy rhythm.

**HARD LIMIT ON TEXT PER COMPONENT:**
- 1 header (3-5 words)
- 1 hook sentence (under 15 words)
- 1-2 body sentences (under 20 words each)
- 1 hero number
- 2-3 callout labels (2-4 words each)
- TOTAL: under 80 words per component section. If you exceed this, CUT.

**Serif for authority, sans-serif for ease**. Headers in serif. Body in sans-serif.

**If text doesn't fit with generous white space around it, the text is too long. Cut it.**

### Key / Legend
Include a small color key near the top or bottom, styled as a museum-label legend:
- Small pigment swatches (circles or small squares) with component names in matching color
- Bordered with a thin rule line — like a map legend
- Helps the reader decode the color system at a glance

## Composition

### Layout Zones
1. **Title cartouche** (top 10-12%): Formal scholarly header with elegant serif title, ornamental rules, subtitle, attribution. Slightly deeper parchment tint.
2. **Illustration zone** (center 50-60%): The central deconstructed subject in RICH PIGMENT MULTI-COLOR on warm parchment, with color-coded callout lines extending into margins
3. **Narrative zones** (sides and bottom): Color-coded section text blocks in serif type. Each section anchored by a COLORED HEADER in that component's pigment.
4. **Detail zones** (optional): Zoomed-in views rendered as small inset vignettes, secondary diagrams, cross-section details
5. **Footer strip** (bottom 5-8%): Stats bar, sources, legend/key — rendered in a slightly darker parchment band with thin border rules

### Information Flow
1. Scholarly title draws the reader in with authority
2. The colorful illustration on warm parchment grabs attention — it's beautiful and informative
3. A specific pigment color catches the eye (e.g., the vermillion engine)
4. They scan for the matching vermillion text header
5. They read the description in warm brown serif
6. They repeat for other colors — the system is intuitive

### Parchment & Texture
- The warm parchment background is ESSENTIAL to the museum feel
- The illustration should sit ON the parchment — not floating above it
- Text looks TYPESET into the paper — not overlaid
- Subtle paper grain and aging give depth and authenticity
- The colored components are the VIVID elements that pop against the warm neutral ground
